01Sketch

Sketch before formula

A rough graph prevents wrong limits and wrong upper-lower order.

  • CBSE writing: Draw a neat labeled diagram.
  • JEE focus: Sketching identifies faster integration direction.
  • Common trap: Formula without diagram often flips the area.
02Limits

Intersection points

Solve curves together to find boundaries of the shaded region.

  • CBSE writing: Show equation solving for limits.
  • JEE focus: Wrong intersection points ruin the answer.
  • Common trap: Do not assume textbook-looking limits.
03Vertical

Area under y=f(x)

For area above x-axis, integrate f(x) over x-limits.

  • CBSE writing: Use positive area, split if curve crosses axis.
  • JEE focus: Signed integral may need absolute handling.
  • Common trap: Area cannot be negative.
04Between

Upper minus lower

Area between y=f(x) and y=g(x) is integral of top minus bottom.

  • CBSE writing: Mention which curve is above.
  • JEE focus: Curves can switch order; split interval if needed.
  • Common trap: Do not subtract in random order.
05Horizontal

Right minus left

If horizontal strips are simpler, integrate x_right - x_left with respect to y.

  • CBSE writing: State y-limits clearly.
  • JEE focus: Many parabola-line regions are easier this way.
  • Common trap: Do not mix x and y limits.
06Symmetry

Symmetric regions

Use symmetry about axes to compute one part and multiply.

  • CBSE writing: Explain symmetry in one sentence.
  • JEE focus: Symmetry reduces algebra.
  • Common trap: Only multiply when the region is truly symmetric.
07Axis

Area with axes

When axes form boundaries, include x=0, y=0, or both in the sketch.

  • CBSE writing: Label axes as boundaries.
  • JEE focus: Intercepts often give limits.
  • Common trap: Missing axis boundary changes the region.
08Sign

Positive final area

Area is always non-negative even if a signed integral is negative.

  • CBSE writing: Use absolute value or correct order.
  • JEE focus: Split when graph crosses the axis.
  • Common trap: Negative area is not a final answer.

Formula sheet

High-yield formulas with exact usage conditions.

Use these as quick revision cards before solving examples.

Area

Area under curve

Area = integral_a^b y dx

Vertical strip area under y=f(x).

  • Example: Area under y=x from 0 to 2 is 2.
  • Trap: Use absolute area if below x-axis.
Between

Between curves

Area = integral_a^b (upper - lower) dx

Subtract lower y-value from upper y-value.

  • Example: Between y=x and y=x^2 on [0,1].
  • Trap: Order matters.
Horizontal

Horizontal strip formula

Area = integral_c^d (right - left) dy

Useful when x is a function of y.

  • Example: Sideways parabolas often need dy.
  • Trap: Limits must be y-values.
Axis

Area bounded by x-axis

Area = integral_a^b |f(x)| dx

Absolute value handles sign changes.

  • Example: If f is below x-axis, area is negative integral times -1.
  • Trap: Signed integral is not always area.
Symmetry

Y-axis symmetry

Area total = 2 x area on one side

Use for even-looking regions.

  • Example: Circle or parabola symmetric about y-axis.
  • Trap: Check full region symmetry.
Circle

Circle upper half

x^2+y^2=a^2 gives y=sqrt(a^2-x^2)

Upper semicircle function.

  • Example: Area upper semicircle is pi a^2 / 2.
  • Trap: Choose positive branch for upper half.
Parabola

Standard parabola

y^2=4ax => x=y^2/(4a)

Often easier with dy integration.

  • Example: Right-opening parabola uses horizontal strips.
  • Trap: Do not solve for wrong branch.
Line

Line intercept form

x/a + y/b = 1

Intercepts give triangle boundaries.

  • Example: Area with axes is ab/2.
  • Trap: Intercept signs matter.

Derivation bank

Proofs that can be reproduced in exams.

Open a proof, follow the steps, and check the final result.

Area between two curves
  1. Take a thin vertical strip of width dx.
  2. Its height is upper y minus lower y.
  3. Small area is (upper-lower)dx.
  4. Add strips by integrating from left intersection to right intersection.

Final: integral upper minus lower.

Shortcut: Use vertical strip height.

Warning: Split if curves cross.

Horizontal strip formula
  1. Take a thin horizontal strip of height dy.
  2. Its width is right x minus left x.
  3. Small area is (right-left)dy.
  4. Integrate from lower y-boundary to upper y-boundary.

Final: integral right minus left dy.

Shortcut: Use horizontal strip width.

Warning: Limits are y-values.

Area under x-axis
  1. If f(x) is below the x-axis, f(x) is negative.
  2. The signed integral is negative.
  3. Physical area is positive.
  4. Use absolute value or reverse sign.

Final: Area uses absolute value.

Shortcut: Area is positive measure.

Warning: Signed integral can be negative.

Symmetry area shortcut
  1. Identify line of symmetry.
  2. Show the two parts have equal area.
  3. Integrate only one part.
  4. Multiply by 2 or 4 as appropriate.

Final: Compute one symmetric part and multiply.

Shortcut: Use axis symmetry.

Warning: Region and boundary must both be symmetric.

Practice bank

Exam-style questions with answer reveal.

Filter by keyword, try the question first, then reveal the answer and trap.

CBSEEasy

1. Area under y=x from 0 to 2?

Answer: 2

integral_0^2 x dx = [x^2/2]_0^2 = 2.

Trap: Do not forget limits.

BetweenMedium

2. Area between y=x and y=x^2 from 0 to 1?

Answer: 1/6

On [0,1], x is above x^2, so integral_0^1 (x-x^2)dx = 1/2-1/3=1/6.

Trap: Upper curve is y=x.

SignMedium

3. If f(x)<0 on [a,b], how do you find area with x-axis?

Answer: - integral_a^b f(x)dx

Area is positive while signed integral is negative.

Trap: Area cannot be negative.

LimitsMedium

4. How do you find limits for area between two curves?

Answer: Solve their intersection points

Set the curve equations equal.

Trap: Do not guess limits from memory.

HorizontalMedium

5. When is dy integration better?

Answer: When horizontal strips give simpler right-left expressions

Use x as functions of y.

Trap: Do not mix dx formulas with y-limits.

SymmetryEasy

6. If a region is symmetric about y-axis, what can you do?

Answer: Compute right half and double it

Both halves have equal area.

Trap: Check the whole region is symmetric.

CircleHard

7. Area of upper semicircle x^2+y^2=a^2?

Answer: pi a^2 / 2

It is half area of circle radius a.

Trap: Upper half only.

LineMedium

8. Area bounded by x/a + y/b = 1 and coordinate axes?

Answer: ab/2

Intercepts are a and b, forming a right triangle.

Trap: Assumes positive intercepts.

TrapMedium

9. Can area between curves be found by lower minus upper?

Answer: No, unless you take absolute value

Standard vertical strip height is upper-lower.

Trap: Wrong order gives negative value.

ParabolaHard

10. For y^2=4ax, should x be written in terms of y for horizontal strips?

Answer: Yes, x=y^2/(4a)

Right-opening parabola often uses dy.

Trap: Use correct branch and bounds.

CBSEMedium

11. Why should a graph be drawn first?

Answer: To identify region, limits, and upper/lower curve

The diagram drives the integral setup.

Trap: Skipping diagram causes order errors.

JEEHard

12. What if two curves cross inside the interval?

Answer: Split the integral at crossing point

Upper and lower functions change roles.

Trap: One integral may subtract in wrong order.

Core formulas

  • Area under curve: Area = integral_a^b y dx
  • Between curves: Area = integral_a^b (upper - lower) dx
  • Horizontal strip formula: Area = integral_c^d (right - left) dy
  • Area bounded by x-axis: Area = integral_a^b |f(x)| dx
  • Y-axis symmetry: Area total = 2 x area on one side
  • Circle upper half: x^2+y^2=a^2 gives y=sqrt(a^2-x^2)

Common traps

  • Always draw and label the region.
  • Area is upper minus lower for vertical strips.
  • For horizontal strips use right minus left.
  • Intersections give limits.
  • Area is never negative.
  • Split when curves cross.
